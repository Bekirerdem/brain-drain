# The thesis

For a solo builder in an 11-day window, the public commit timeline is the strongest signal of quality and momentum a judge can read. I optimise for:

1. **Day 0 lands a meaningful scaffold** — README that pitches, `docs/architecture.md` with a Mermaid diagram, `docs/roadmap.md` with the 11-day plan, env templates, MIT license. Not a placeholder.
2. **One meaningful commit per substantive change** — atomic, with a Conventional message that says WHY.
3. **Public from Day 0, not Day 9.** Switching from private to public on submission day looks rushed; public from the first push tells the judge you trust the work.

## Sponsor narrative-fit

Frontier 2026's heavy bias is agentic payments (~$135K bounty pool across x402, Phantom Cash, CDP Embedded, AgentPay). Brain Drain hits all four with one MVP. I name them explicitly in the README "Sponsor bounties targeted" table, not just claim "we hit them".

## The differentiation cut

Cypherpunk 2025 already produced two x402 winners (MCPay → Frames, CORBITS). The judges have x402-fatigue. My positioning sentence is:

> "MCPay monetises the tools AI agents call. Brain Drain monetises the humans behind those tools."

Same protocol, inverted supply side. The differentiation is human-as-seller + Phantom Cash as the natural payout surface — neither MCPay nor CORBITS leans on Phantom Cash.

## What I don't do

- Don't try to be Grand Champion (those win on novel primitives like Ore, Reflect, TAPEDRIVE — out of scope for an 11-day MVP).
- Don't chase Public Goods unless the project is genuinely a public good (mine is, partially).
- Don't engineer for the judges in a way that compromises post-hackathon survival. The MVP must keep being useful even after the prize is decided.
